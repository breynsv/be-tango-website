History of Argentine Tango

Sonja February 1, 2025 3 min read

At the end of the 19th century, the dances and music of black slaves, seasonal workers, and European immigrants merged with those of gauchos in the Río de la Plata region, giving birth to one of the world's most passionate and captivating dance forms.

Origins in the Río de la Plata

Argentine tango emerged from a unique cultural melting pot. In the late 1800s, Buenos Aires and Montevideo were bustling ports receiving waves of European immigrants, particularly from Italy and Spain. These newcomers brought their musical traditions, which blended with the existing influences of African slaves and the rural gaucho culture.

The early tango was a raw, earthy expression of life in the working-class neighborhoods. It was danced primarily in conventillos (tenement houses), bars, and brothels—places where the marginalized found community and expression through music and dance.

Early Development (1890-1900)

In these early days, tango was predominantly a male practice. Women were scarce in the dance halls, so men would dance together to practice steps and develop their technique. This period saw the emergence of distinctive movements like the canyengue—characterized by close embrace and rhythmic walking.

The musical accompaniment was minimal but expressive: typically a guitar, violin, or flute provided the soundtrack for dancers to explore this new form of expression. The music itself was evolving, incorporating elements of candombe (African rhythms), milonga (from the rural pampas), and European salon music.

The Golden Era (1930-1950)

The época de oro—the golden age—represents tango's peak in Argentine society. During these decades, tango transcended its working-class origins to become a national phenomenon embraced by all social classes.

Radio broadcasting played a crucial role in tango's widespread adoption. Legendary orchestras led by Carlos Gardel, Aníbal Troilo, and Osvaldo Pugliese filled the airwaves, making tango accessible to millions. Grand ballrooms and dance halls opened throughout Buenos Aires, hosting elaborate milongas where dressed-up couples danced until dawn.

This era also saw the refinement of tango music and dance technique. The orchestras grew larger and more sophisticated, incorporating bandoneóns (the distinctive tango accordion) as the central instrument. Dance styles diversified, with different neighborhoods developing their own characteristic approaches to the embrace and movement.

European Adoption

Tango's journey to Europe began in the early 20th century but faced significant resistance. The Catholic Church and medical authorities condemned it as immoral and unhealthy due to its sensual close embrace and suggestive movements.

To gain acceptance in European ballrooms, a sanitized "ballroom tango" was created—more upright, structured, and stripped of the improvisational intimacy that defined the Argentine style. This version found success in Paris, London, and other European capitals during the 1910s and 1920s.

However, the authentic Argentine style didn't resurface in Europe until the 1970s, when a new generation discovered the raw passion and complexity of the original dance. This revival was partly sparked by the touring show "Tango Argentino" in the 1980s, which reintroduced European audiences to the genuine article.

Modern Revival and UNESCO Recognition

Today, tango thrives globally. Contemporary tango culture features dedicated dance schools, weekly milongas, international festivals, marathons, and "tango tourism" throughout Europe, Asia, and the Americas. Buenos Aires maestros regularly tour the world, teaching and performing.

In 2009, UNESCO inscribed Argentine Tango on the Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity, recognizing its profound cultural significance. The dance that began in the humble neighborhoods of Buenos Aires has become a global phenomenon, connecting people across continents through its universal language of connection, improvisation, and musical expression.

Whether you're dancing in a traditional milonga in Buenos Aires or a modern tango hall in Brussels, you're participating in a living tradition that spans more than a century—a dance that continues to evolve while honoring its rich, complex history.
