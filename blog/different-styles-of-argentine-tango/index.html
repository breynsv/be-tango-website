The Different Styles of Argentine Tango

Sonja January 28, 2025 8 min read

Argentine tango refers to a broad spectrum of dance styles. Yet most dancers resist categorical labels, preferring to describe their approach as "the tango of their neighbourhood or city." This diversity reflects the organic evolution of tango across different communities and eras.

1. Canyengue (1920s-1930s)

Canyengue represents one of the earliest recognizable tango styles, characterized by its distinctive rhythmic energy and intimate connection. The name derives from Lunfardo, the colorful slang of Buenos Aires working-class neighborhoods.

Key Characteristics:

  • Short, staccato steps with bent knees
  • Very close embrace with partners often dancing chest-to-chest
  • Music in 2/4 rhythmic tempo
  • Playful, bouncy quality in the movement

Notable orchestras from this era include Francisco Canaro (famous for "El Chamuyo") and Roberto Firpo. The style reflects the raw, spontaneous energy of early tango culture.

2. Tango Orillero

Tango Orillero—literally "tango from the outskirts"—emerged from the working-class neighborhoods on the edges of Buenos Aires. This style was revived and popularized by Rodolfo Cieri around 1990.

Unlike the hunched posture of canyengue, orillero features an upright posture combined with playful, expressive movements. Dancers maintain a more open frame while incorporating theatrical elements that reflect the street culture where tango was born.

3. Tango de Salon

Tango de Salon emerged as tango moved from the streets into the elegant ballrooms of Buenos Aires. This became the social style par excellence—elegant, refined, and suitable for crowded dance floors.

Key Features:

  • Elegant, smooth movements suitable for social dancing
  • Closed embrace (abrazo cerrado)
  • Circular navigation around the dance floor
  • Emphasis on musicality and connection

The golden age orchestras of Carlos Di Sarli, Miguel Calo, and Osvaldo Pugliese created the sophisticated sound that defined this style. Interestingly, Christine Denniston's research in The Meaning of Tango reveals that although dance styles varied widely during the golden age, dancers simply called what they danced "Tango de Salon."

4. Tango Milonguero (Estilo del Centro)

Tango Milonguero, also known as Estilo del Centro (downtown style), developed in the crowded milongas of central Buenos Aires. When dance floors became packed, dancers adapted by minimizing their movements and maintaining constant chest contact.

Characteristics:

  • Very close embrace with constant chest connection
  • Compact, efficient movements for crowded floors
  • Walking-based with minimal large movements
  • Strong emphasis on rhythmic precision

Music preference leans toward rhythmic pieces by Juan D'Arienzo, Tanturi, and Biagi. The legendary Pedro "Tete" Rusconi popularized this style globally and famously influenced choreographer Pina Bausch.

5. Tango Villa Urquiza

Named after a neighborhood in Buenos Aires, Tango Villa Urquiza flourished in the 1940s-1950s. This style is often called "salon-style tango" internationally.

Distinctive Elements:

  • Upright posture with a subtle V-position
  • Independent hip rotation allowing for elegant leg movements
  • More space in the embrace compared to milonguero
  • Smooth, flowing movements with sophisticated footwork

This style represents the refinement of social tango, balancing elegance with the intimacy of close connection.

6. Tango Nuevo

Tango Nuevo represents the modern, experimental approach to tango that emerged in the 1990s. Pioneers like Gustavo Naveira and Mariano "Chicho" Frumboli revolutionized the dance by questioning traditional structures and exploring new possibilities.

Key Innovations:

  • Open embrace allowing greater freedom of movement
  • Volcadas (off-axis movements) and creative figures
  • Emphasis on individual expression and creativity
  • Integration of elements from other dance forms

Other pioneers include Norberto "El Pulpo" Esbrés, Fabián Salas, Sebastian Arce, and Mariana Montes. This style appeals to dancers seeking to push boundaries while maintaining tango's essential improvisational nature.

7. Tango Fantasia

Developed in the 1950s and popularized by the legendary Juan Carlos Copes (who passed away in 2021), Tango Fantasia is fully choreographed stage performance tango.

Performance Characteristics:

  • Fully choreographed sequences designed for audience viewing
  • Incorporates ballet elements and acrobatic moves
  • Often performed in theatrical settings
  • Requires specialized footwear (typically 9 cm heels for women)

This style is designed for the stage rather than social dancing, prioritizing visual impact and dramatic effect.

8. Tango Escenario

Tango Escenario is another performance-oriented style specifically designed for stage shows and theatrical productions. Like Tango Fantasia, it emphasizes visual spectacle and choreographed sequences, but may incorporate elements from various tango styles depending on the artistic vision.

9. Candombé Influence

While not a tango style per se, Candombé deserves mention as a crucial influence on tango's development. This African musical tradition arrived in South America through the slave trade in the 17th-18th centuries, particularly affecting the Rio de la Plata region.

Black communities played a crucial role in tango's formation. Buenos Aires contained approximately 20,000 African residents by the end of the 18th century, and their rhythmic traditions became woven into the fabric of what would become tango.

The Reality of Tango Styles

Despite these categorical labels, historical research by Christine Denniston reveals an important truth: during the golden age (Época de Oro), dancers with widely varying styles all simply called what they danced "Tango de Salon."

Today's style names often represent modern attempts to categorize what was historically a more fluid practice. Most experienced dancers don't strictly adhere to one style but draw from multiple influences depending on the music, their partner, and the social context of the dance floor.

The beauty of Argentine tango lies in this diversity and adaptability—it's a living art form that continues to evolve while honoring its rich, multifaceted history.
