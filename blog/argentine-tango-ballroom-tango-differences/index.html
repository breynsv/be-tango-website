Argentine Tango & Ballroom Tango: Similarities and Differences

Sven February 3, 2021 6 min read

Though descended from common roots, Argentine tango and ballroom tango represent fundamentally different approaches to dance. While one prioritizes improvisation and personal expression, the other emphasizes standardized choreography and competitive performance.

A Brief History

Argentine tango emerged in late 19th-century Buenos Aires and Montevideo, blending dance traditions from enslaved Africans, European immigrants, and local gauchos. During early tango's infancy (1890-1900), primarily men developed dance steps in "canyengue" style, with minimal musical accompaniment.

The dance gained widespread popularity in the early 20th century as orchestras grew and milongas (tango venues) flourished nightly. The 1930s-1950s represented tango's golden era, with radio amplifying its reach across Argentina's population.

European adoption faced considerable resistance from religious and medical establishments, which viewed tango as vulgar. A refined European version eventually emerged, creating ballroom tango. The original Argentine form didn't reach Europe until the 1970s, subsequently becoming wildly popular through dance schools, festivals, and "tango tourism."

Argentine Tango: A Living, Evolving Dance

A Dance "That Moves"

Argentine tango continues evolving today—it remains unfinished artistically. Unlike ballroom tango's standardized approach, Argentine tango maintains its original improvisation-centered character.

Posture, Embrace, Abrazo

The closed embrace (abrazo cerrado) defines traditional Argentine tango, while the open embrace (abrazo abierto) permits complex figure execution. The body position forms an inverted "∧" shape, with upper bodies touching but knees and hips remaining separated. Arms are "carried" from shoulder joints via the back, with elbows positioned before the hips rather than alongside them.

Improvisation: The Heart of Argentine Tango

Improvisation fundamentally distinguishes Argentine tango from ballroom tango. Rather than predetermined choreography, Argentine tango functions as "non-verbal dialogue between leader and follower, in which physical communication and body contact between the dance partners play an important role."

Partners respond intuitively to movements, adapting when leads diverge from intention. This responsiveness creates shared problem-solving within the dance.

Basic Elements, Not Choreography

While beginners learn foundational figures and postures, each movement permits variations in direction, rhythm, and speed. Dancers may choose to interpret melody rather than beat, allowing leaders to decide pacing and timing. Followers contribute significantly through adorning breaks with embellishments (adornos).

Unlike ballroom tango's standardized rules, Argentine tango lacks regulations governing foot rolling or posture correctness. This flexibility enables personal style development and makes objective judging extremely difficult—consequently, few official competitions exist.

Six Different Styles

Six distinctive Argentine tango styles have emerged: Canyengue, Orillero, Milonguero, Tango Salon, Tango Fantasia, and Tango Nuevo, each reflecting different historical periods and evolutionary stages.

Ballroom Tango: Precision and Competition

Origin and Development

Ballroom tango originated from Argentine tango but was refined and standardized for European audiences. Hollywood films popularized this polished, less sensual version. English influence transformed it into a competitive, standardized dance within the five standard ballroom dances.

Music and Rhythm

Ballroom tango maintains "strict tempo" of approximately 31-33 bars per minute, creating less sensuality than original tango. Characteristic staccato movements and head accents performed by the follower mark specific musical moments.

Body Position and Technique

Ballroom dancers maintain slightly bent knees and open arm frames with distance between upper bodies, forming a "V" shape. Leadership originates from hip joints with stiff arm posture, contrasting sharply with Argentine tango's relaxed approach.

Two Competitive Styles

Ballroom tango split into "American style" and "International Style," with International Style dominating competitive contexts worldwide. Standardized handbooks enable objective jury assessment of participants' techniques and movements.

The Fundamental Difference

As one instructor notes: "nothing is laid down in a manual, there is only the certainty that one dance partner will lead another across the dance floor." This encapsulates the philosophical distinction between these dance forms.

Argentine tango prioritizes improvisation, flexible interpretation, and personal expression within basic structural elements. Ballroom tango emphasizes standardized choreography, precise technique, and competitive performance.

Interestingly, ballroom-trained dancers learn Argentine tango steps quickly but require substantial effort relearning posture and musical interpretation, as the frameworks differ substantially.
