Tango glossary:
key terms explained

From abrazo to yumba — a complete guide through the terminology of Argentine tango. Whether you are just starting out or have danced for years, this glossary helps you understand and name the steps, the embrace, the movement, and the music.

Posture and connection — the fundamentals

What is the absolute foundation of Argentine tango? It’s not your feet. After 15 years of teaching here in Brussels, I can tell you exactly what matters: your spine and the connection you build with your partner. Forget the flashy YouTube figures. If your posture and embrace fail, nothing works. That’s why we relentlessly drill these basics at BE-TANGO.

  • Eje Your eje is your personal axis. Listen to me carefully: you carry your own weight. Always. A stable axis means you are an independent dancer, not a piece of furniture for your partner to lean heavily on. You dance with them, not on them.
  • Apilado This is the classic, leaning embrace you'll see on crowded local dance floors. You and your partner share a single axis. It feels like you’re falling, but you don’t, because the connection is absolute. Pure trust.
  • Disociación Here’s the mechanical secret of tango. It’s separating your upper body from your lower body. Twist your torso while keeping your hips steady. Without disociación, ochos and sacadas are physically impossible. Period.
  • Marco Leaders, this is your job. The marco is your frame—your arms, your back, your chest. It’s how you transmit your intention without a single word. Keep it firm but never stiff. You are inviting, not shoving. Think of it as a clear, polite conversation.

Tango doesn't happen in your shoes. It happens in your spine, and in the space you share with another human being.

The embrace — abrazo and its variations

How do you hold your partner in tango? The abrazo (embrace) isn't just a frame; it’s a living, breathing dialogue. In my classes, I teach that there is no single "correct" posture—only the embrace that works for you and your partner in this exact moment, to this exact song.

  • Abrazo cerrado The close embrace. Chest-to-chest. This is the beating heart of social tango. It’s what you’ll use 90% of the time navigating crowded floors from Saint-Gilles to the Marolles. It’s for deep connection, not showing off.
  • Abrazo abierto The open embrace. You leave space between you for bigger, dramatic movements. Great for stage performances. But try this on a packed Saturday night at La Tentation? You’ll catch an elbow and annoy the room. Keep it off the social floor.
  • Colgada You both lean away from a shared center, spinning like a top. It feels incredible, like flying. But it requires absolute core control and perfect technique. I won't teach you this in your first month. You need to earn it.
  • Volcada The opposite of a colgada. The leader becomes a solid tree trunk, supporting the follower as she leans forward to paint a lazy, sweeping arc with her free leg. It’s a beautiful moment of surrender.

Which embrace should you use? The music and the traffic on the floor will dictate it. Stop overthinking. Listen to the orchestra, feel your partner's chest, and adapt.

Movement terms — steps, turns, and embellishments

What are the actual moves of tango? This is our shared vocabulary. These are the terms I'll be calling out in the studio, and the movements you’ll be dancing all night at the Brussels milongas.

  • Ocho The figure-eight. The follower traces an eight on the floor with her toes, either backwards (ocho atrás) or forwards (ocho adelante). The secret isn't in your shoes—it’s in the pivot, driven by your disociación.
  • Molinete The windmill. The follower dances a continuous circle around the leader. We hammer this early on because it’s a core pattern you will use with every single partner you ever have.
  • Sacada A displacement. You step into the exact space your partner's leg just vacated. Let me be blunt: do NOT kick. This is an illusion built on precise timing, not brute force. You take the space; you don't attack it.
  • Gancho The hook. A sharp, rhythmic flick of your leg around your partner's. Executed well, it's brilliant. Executed poorly, it's a weapon. A sloppy gancho is the fastest way to bruise someone and ruin your reputation on the Belgian scene.
  • Parada The stop. The leader gently blocks the follower's foot, creating a delicious, suspended pause. Time stops. Then, she elegantly steps over his foot (a pasada) and the dance breathes again.
  • Adorno Embellishments. A quick heel tap, a slow caress of your foot against your partner's calf. This is your personal signature. It’s how you say, "This is how I hear the music."
  • Amague A tease. The leader suggests a step, then instantly changes direction. It’s a playful, cheeky way to interpret a syncopated beat.
  • Boleo The whip. A sharp change of direction from the leader snaps the follower's free leg into an arc. Leaders: a boleo is a reaction, a consequence of your lead. If she has to consciously "kick," you led it wrong. It’s physics.

4

Musical terms — rhythm, pulse, and musicality

How do you dance to tango music? You listen first, and step second. If you aren't listening to the orchestra, you're just doing a complicated walk. The music is your boss. It dictates everything.

  • Compás The beat. The relentless pulse. Before I let you do a single ocho, you have to find the compás and walk to it. Just walk. It is the hardest and most important thing in tango.
  • Tanda A set of three or four songs by the same orchestra. When you invite someone, you commit to the whole tanda. Walking away after one song is a massive insult. Don't be that person.
  • Cortina The curtain. A snippet of non-tango music—salsa, rock, whatever—played between tandas. It means the set is over. Say merci or dank u, clear the floor, and look for your next partner.
  • Vals Tango waltz. It’s in 3/4 time and feels completely different. No dramatic pauses here. Vals is a flowing river. You glide continuously across the floor.
  • Milonga (music) The fast, earthy, syncopated grandparent of tango. It requires tiny, quick, rhythmic steps. My golden rule for milonga: if you are sweating and smiling, you are doing it right.
  • Yumba You’ll recognize it instantly. The heavy, dramatic, dragging beat made famous by Osvaldo Pugliese: YUM-ba... YUM-ba. You cannot dance this timidly. You have to ground yourself and dance with pure power.
  • Pausa The silence between the notes. The best dancers in Brussels aren't the ones doing endless tricks; they are the masters of the pause. Don't be afraid to just stand still and hold your partner. Let the music breathe.

Your feet don't lead. Your heart and your ears lead.

05

The milonga — social terms and etiquette

What exactly is a milonga? It’s our social club. It’s not just a dance party; it is a micro-society with strict, beautiful codes. Learn the etiquette, and you can walk into any event in the world—from a relaxed Sunday afternoon in Ixelles to a historic hall in Buenos Aires—and belong.

The most crucial rule is the cabeceo. This is how we invite each other. You catch someone's eye across the room. You nod. They nod back. Then, and only then, do you approach. It’s discreet, it avoids public rejection, and it is non-negotiable in my book.

Think of the dance floor as the Brussels ring road, but moving counter-clockwise. This is the línea de baile (line of dance). Stay in your lane and flow with the traffic. Never step backwards against the current. The outer edge is the fast lane for traveling; the center is a parking lot. Avoid the center.

Together, we form the ronda—the moving circle of couples. Being a good dancer means being a good citizen of the ronda. Protect your partner from collisions, watch the couple ahead of you, and don't hog the floor. Respect the ronda, and the milonga will welcome you with open arms.
