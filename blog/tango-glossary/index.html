Tango glossary:
key terms explained

From abrazo to yumba — a complete guide through the terminology of Argentine tango. Whether you are just starting out or have danced for years, this glossary helps you understand and name the steps, the embrace, the movement, and the music.

1

Posture and connection — the fundamentals

What's the first thing you need to learn in tango? It's not the steps. Forget the fancy figures you've seen online. Tango starts in your spine. Your posture and the connection you create with your partner are everything. Get this wrong, and nothing else will work. It’s the first thing we hammer home in our classes here at BE-TANGO, because it’s the most important.

  • Eje Your *eje* is your personal axis. Your balance. Let me be clear: you are always responsible for your own weight. Always. A stable axis means you are a dancer, not a piece of furniture for your partner to lean on. You dance *with* them, not *on* them.
  • Apilado This is that classic, leaning embrace you see in the crowded milongas. You and your partner create a shared axis between you. It feels like you’re about to fall, but you don’t, because the connection is total. That’s pure trust, pure tango.
  • Disociación Here’s the magic trick of tango. It’s the separation of your upper and lower body—the ability to twist your torso against your hips. Without this, ochos and sacadas are physically impossible. It's as simple as that.
  • Marco Leaders, this is on you. The *marco* is your frame—your arms, your chest. It’s how you communicate the dance without words. It must be clear and firm, but never tense or rigid. You are guiding, not pushing. Think of it as a silent conversation.

People think tango begins with the feet. They're wrong. It begins with your spine, and with the connection you build with another person.

The embrace — abrazo and its variations

So, what is the embrace? For me, the abrazo *is* tango. It’s not just holding someone. It’s a living, breathing dialogue that changes with every partner, every song. There is no single "correct" embrace, only the one that works for the two of you, right now.

  • Abrazo cerrado The close embrace. Chest-to-chest, heart-to-heart. This is the soul of social tango. It’s what you’ll use 99% of the time on a crowded floor in Brussels, from Saint-Gilles to the city centre. It’s for feeling the dance, not for showing off.
  • Abrazo abierto The open embrace. This gives you space for bigger, more dramatic movements—perfect for stage tango. But on a packed Saturday night at La Tentation or a milonga at Art-Base? You’ll get some sharp looks, maybe even an elbow. Use it with extreme care on the social floor.
  • Colgada This is a thrilling move where you both lean away from a shared axis, spinning like a top. It feels like flying, I promise. But it demands absolute trust and a rock-solid technique. We won't be teaching this in your first week.
  • Volcada The opposite of a colgada. Here, the leader becomes a solid anchor, supporting the follower as she leans forward and paints a lazy arc with her free leg. It’s a moment of complete surrender and trust.

So which one do you choose? Don't overthink it. The dance will tell you. Listen to the music, feel the space on the floor, and above all, connect with your partner. The right embrace is the one that feels honest for that specific tanda.

3

Movement terms — steps, turns, and embellishments

Alright, you have your posture, you have your embrace. Now we can talk about the steps. This is the vocabulary of tango. These are the words I'll be shouting in class and the moves you’ll see all night long at the Brussels milongas.

  • Ocho The famous "eight." The follower draws a figure-eight on the floor with her feet. It can be backwards (ocho atrás) or forwards (ocho adelante). But the secret isn't in the feet. It’s in the pivot, which comes from your disociación. You see? It all connects.
  • Molinete The "windmill." The follower dances a circular path around the leader. This is one of the first full sequences we teach because it’s a fundamental conversation you will have with every partner for the rest of your tango life.
  • Sacada A "displacement." You step into the space your partner's leg just left. I need to be very clear about this: it is NOT a kick. It is about timing and precision, not force. You *take* the space; you don't attack it.
  • Gancho The "hook." A fast, sharp hook of one leg around your partner's. In the right hands, it's brilliant. In the wrong hands, it's a disaster. Please, for the love of tango, do this with control! A wild gancho is the fastest way to injure your partner and get a bad reputation on the scene.
  • Parada The "stop." The leader gently blocks the follower's foot with his own, creating a perfect, delicious pause. It’s a moment where time stops. Then, she elegantly steps over his foot (a pasada) to continue the dance.
  • Adorno The "embellishments." These are the little flourishes that make the dance yours. A tap of the heel, a caress of the foot against your partner's leg. This is how you add your personality, how you sign your name on the music.
  • Amague A tease, a fake-out. The leader suggests one thing, then changes direction. It’s a playful way to interpret the music, to add some spice and surprise to the dance.
  • Boleo The "whip." A sharp change of direction from the leader sends the follower's free leg into a dynamic arc. A boleo is a *consequence*, a reaction. If a follower feels she has to 'kick' her leg, the lead was wrong. It’s pure physics, not muscle.

4

Musical terms — rhythm, pulse, and musicality

Let me be very clear: if you aren't listening to the music, you are not dancing tango. You are just walking around in a complicated way. The music is your guide. It's your boss. It's your third partner in the dance. Learning to truly *hear* it is everything.

  • Compás The beat. The pulse. The heartbeat of the music. Before you learn a single fancy step, you must learn to find the compás and simply walk to it. Walk, walk, walk. It’s the first and last lesson in tango.
  • Tanda A set of three or four songs by the same orchestra. You invite someone to dance for the whole tanda. Why? Because it gives you time to build a real connection. Leaving after one song is considered rude. Don't do it.
  • Cortina The "curtain." That short piece of non-tango music played between tandas. That's your signal. Thank your partner, clear the floor, and use the cabeceo to find your next dance.
  • Vals The Argentine waltz. It’s in 3/4 time, and the feeling is completely different. It should feel like you're gliding, flying across the floor. Forget the dramatic pauses of tango; vals is all about continuous, flowing movement.
  • Milonga (music) The fast, happy, mischievous cousin of tango. The rhythm is syncopated and it demands small, quick, playful steps. My rule is simple: if you're dancing milonga and you're not smiling, you're doing it wrong.
  • Yumba You'll know it the moment you hear it. That heavy, percussive, almost brutal beat from the great Osvaldo Pugliese: *YUM-ba... YUM-ba*. It is pure power. You can't be timid with this music; you have to dance it with conviction.
  • Pausa The pause. The silence between the notes. For me, this is where the soul of tango truly lives. The best dancers I know are masters of the pause. They know when *not* to move. Don't be afraid to just stand still, breathe, and listen. It can be the most powerful moment in your entire dance.

Don't dance with your feet. Dance with your heart. And your heart must listen to the music.

05

The milonga — social terms and etiquette

What exactly is a milonga? It’s our social club, our community hall, our sacred space. It’s not just a party. It's a gathering with its own codes and traditions. Learn these, and you can walk into any tango event—from a practilonga here in Saint-Gilles to a grand hall in Buenos Aires—and feel right at home.

The most important code is the cabeceo. This is how you invite someone to dance. You make eye contact across the room. You nod. They nod back. Only then do you walk over. It's elegant, it's respectful, and it saves everyone from the pain of a public refusal. You must learn to use it.

Think of the dance floor as a river, always flowing in one direction: counter-clockwise. This is the línea de baile, the line of dance. Your job is to stay in your lane and move with the current. You never, ever go backwards or cut across the middle. The outside lane is for traveling; the inside lanes are for couples who need more space for turns.

All of us, together, create the ronda—that beautiful, moving circle of dancers. It's a collective responsibility. Your job is to be a good citizen of the ronda, not a disruption. Protect your partner, be aware of the couples in front and behind you, and manage your space. When you respect the ronda, you respect everyone in the milonga.
