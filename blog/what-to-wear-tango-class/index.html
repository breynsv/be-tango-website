Gear & Style

What to wear
to a tango class

You don't need a special wardrobe to start tango. But knowing what works — and what to avoid — will make your first classes more comfortable and your dancing more free.

Read the article
BE-TANGO, Brussels

4 min read 24 March 2026

1

For your first class: keep it simple

Just wear comfortable clothes you can move freely in. Don't overthink it. Jeans, trousers, a skirt, a simple T-shirt—they all work perfectly. You just need to be able to step and pivot without fighting your outfit.

Avoid anything overly baggy. I need to see your knees and posture to actually teach you, and you don't want excess fabric tangling up with your partner. Think of it like grabbing a coffee at the Parvis de Saint-Gilles: relaxed, but put-together.

In tango, your body is your instrument. Dress it so you can move.

For footwear, bring flat indoor shoes or just a pair of clean socks. That's it. The absolute worst thing you can wear? Trainers. Rubber soles stick to our dance floor like glue. You won't be able to pivot, it ruins your knees, and it feels awful.

02
2

Shoes: the most important piece of gear

Ask any dancer who has been at it for a month: your shoes change everything. A real pair of tango shoes with suede or leather soles is the only gear investment you actually need. They let you glide across the floor and pivot effortlessly.

For leaders, get a classic leather shoe with a 2-3 cm heel. Yes, a heel. That tiny lift pushes your weight slightly forward onto the balls of your feet—right into your tango axis. Flats work perfectly fine for walking down Avenue Louise, but in tango, they throw your balance backward.

For followers, you can choose elegant flats or heels between 5-8 cm. A heel gives you that beautiful tango leg line, but please, do not rush into stilettos. Balance always beats aesthetics. I constantly tell my students: start with a low, stable heel. Go higher only when your axis feels unbreakable.

What to avoid? Anything with a rubber sole that grips the floor. Skip the flimsy fashion heels with zero ankle support, too. My rule is ruthless: if a shoe hurts, take it off. You need to focus on the music, not your blisters.

03
3

Clothing for regular classes

When you start dancing regularly at our Ixelles studio, switch to breathable fabrics. Tango is a close-embrace dance. You will sweat. Natural fibres like cotton and bamboo, or good sports fabrics, will save you. Avoid cheap synthetics that trap heat.

For followers, skirts with a bit of flow look fantastic and let me see your legwork clearly. Fitted trousers or leggings are great, too. My one strict veto: wide-leg trousers. Your heel will catch in the fabric during an ocho. I see it happen every week.

For leaders, grab a clean shirt or polo and trousers that give your hips room to move. Dress like you're meeting friends for a drink at Café Belga in Flagey. Comfortable, neat, and respectful to your partner.

My ultimate insider tip? Bring a spare shirt. After an hour of intense class, swapping into a fresh, dry top is bliss. Your dance partners will silently thank you. It's a tiny gesture that shows massive respect in the tango community.

04

Dressing for a milonga

A milonga is our social dance party. In Brussels, we treat it as an event. The dress code is smart casual to elegant. You don't need a sequined stage costume, but dressing up shows respect for the music, the organizers, and the people you embrace.

On the milonga floor, proper tango shoes are non-negotiable. You are going to dance for hours. The right soles save your knees and ground your connection. Watch the veterans: they walk into the venue in street shoes and swap them out by the coat rack. That’s how a real tanguero operates.

Treat the evening like a nice dinner out or a night at a jazz club in the centre. It isn't a gala, but it definitely isn't the gym. When you dress with purpose, your posture changes. When you feel good, your tango follows. Simple as that.
